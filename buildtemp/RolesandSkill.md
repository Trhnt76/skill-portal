On Structure
Each role is broken into weighted categories rather than a flat list. This matters for your portal because a single role score needs to reflect that not all skills carry equal importance. A Platform Engineer who maxes out on IaC but has no observability knowledge isn't actually a well-rounded Platform Engineer — the weighting model in Appendix A captures that.
On Sub-Skills
The skills most warranting decomposition were GitHub, Azure DevOps, Kubernetes, Terraform, Scrum, PMBOK, and the AI frameworks. These are all "umbrella" skills in the job market — a job posting that says "GitHub required" could mean anything from basic commits to running Actions pipelines with OIDC authentication. Your portal needs that granularity to produce a meaningful score.
On the Cross-Role Index
This is the piece you'll need to think about architecturally for the web project. If someone rates themselves a 4 on KQL, that score should propagate into their Azure Cloud Engineer score AND their Platform Engineer score without requiring them to rate it twice. The cross-role index gives you the source-of-truth map for that logic.
On 2026 Calibration
Three things I deliberately emphasized for this year's market that wouldn't have appeared in a 2023 list: Model Context Protocol (MCP) as an emerging agent standard, OpenTelemetry replacing proprietary agents as the observability standard, and LLMOps as a distinct discipline from MLOps. These belong in your portal even at an awareness level because they're already showing up in Staff and Platform Engineer job postings.
Immediate Next Decision for Your Web Project
Before you build the scoring interface, you need to decide one thing: does a skill score at Level 3 represent the same value across all roles, or does the role weight shift how much a Level 3 matters? For example, a Level 3 in KQL is table stakes for an Azure Cloud Engineer but is above-average for a Project Manager. The category weighting handles this at the aggregate, but you may want a "role relevance multiplier" per skill for finer precision. That's worth locking down in your data model before you build the UI.
